Mean Household Economics of Malaysia

Household economics in Malaysia reflects the financial well-being and living standards of families across different regions. Over the past few decades, Malaysia has achieved steady income growth and poverty reduction through industrialisation, urban expansion, and improved education access.

Behind Malaysia’s steady rise in income lies a diverse story — where some states thrive while others face persistent inequality.
Overview

Understanding the Indicators

Each indicator highlights a key aspect of Malaysia’s socioeconomic landscape.

  • Mean Income — average household earnings.
  • Mean Expenditure — average household spending patterns.
  • Poverty Rate — proportion of families living below the national poverty line.

Economic Disparities Across Malaysia

The map reveals Malaysia's persistent regional economic imbalance driven by structural and regional factors. High-income states in the central region, particularly Kuala Lumpur and Selangor, continnue to dominate due to urban concentration, diversified industries and advanced infrastructure, which attract invesment and skill labours.

In contrast, East Malaysia, especially Sabah and Sarawak, shows slower income growth due to geographical isolation and infrastructure gaps, leaving them reliant on primary industries like oil, gas and timber.

Meanwhile, states like Johor and Melaka have shown steady progress, benefiting from cross-border trade with Singapore where industrial zones expanded, toursim and port activities are also grown. In constrast, northern states such as Kedah and Perlis maintain moderate income levels, reflecting their dependence on agriculture and smaller urban economies.

Income

Understanding the Income Trends

Malaysia's income and poverty patterns reflect three key turning points:

  • 2007–2014: Steady income growth and falling poverty rates marked post-industrial growth.
  • 2016: Poverty rate spiked due to introduction of GST and inflation pressures.
  • 2020–2022: Both income dropped and poverty rose, highlighting COVID-19's direct economic impact.

Overall, high income usually aligns with lower poverty, but GST and COVID-19 disrupts this pattern and widen disparities.

Income Ranking Dynamics

Despite national growth, the ranking chart shows persistent regional inequality.

  • Putrajaya and Selangor maintain high income levels with minor shifts.
  • Johor shows steady progress, reflecting strong industrial growth.
  • Perlis and Kedah remain in lower ranks but show gradual improvement.
Expenditure

Household Spending Patterns

Malaysia's overall household spending remained relatively stable between 2016 to 2019, then experiencing slight decline in 2022, reflecting impact of COVID-19 pandemic on Malaysia's economy.

The Central Region recorded the highest mean expenditure, supported by higher living costs and income levels in urbanised states like Kuala Lumpur and Selangor.

In contrast, East Malaysia and East Coast regions like Kelantan and Terengganu showed lovwer average expenditure, aligning with their generally lower income levels and more rural composition.

In terms of expenditure categories, Housing & Utilities contributed to the largest share of spending, followed by Restaurants & Accomodations, Food & Beverages and Transport. This indicates that hosuehold budgets are mainly dominated by essential spending while expenditures on Recreation & Culture, Clothing & Footwear and other non essentials remain comparatively smaller.

Overall, this reflects how Malaysian households prioritize necessities despite ongoing cost-of-living pressure and the gradual economic recovery.

Household & Income Inequality

Insights on Household & Income Inequality

The scatterplot illustrates the relationship between mean household income and income inequality (Gini coefficient), revealing persistent disparities among Malaysian states. In earlier years like 2007, states such as Kuala Lumpur, Selangor and Pulau Pinang recorded higher mean household incomes yet their Gini coefficients remain relatively similar to other states. This indicates that income growth in these urbanised regions was not evenly distributed, with wealth concentrated among higher income groups.

In contrast, poorer states like Kelantan, Perlis and Sabah displayed lower mean incomes alongside moderare inequality, reflecting limited economic diversification and slower development progress.

The heatmap further demonstrates a steady rise in the number of households from 2010 to 2024, particularly in urbanised and industrial states like Selangor, Johor and Kuala Lumpur, likley driven by migration and better employment opportunities. Meanwhile, East Malaysian states like Sabah and Sarawak exhibits slower household growth, consistent with their dependence on primary industries and limited infrastructure.

Viewed together, both these visuals emphasise that while Malaysia has achieved nationwide household growth, regional income inequality persists, with urban prosperity coexisting alongside rural economic lag.
